01 The 30-second summary

A micro:bit robot is dropped into a maze it has never seen. Using three laser distance sensors it feels the walls, and a single rule — keep your left hand on the wall — tells it which way to turn at every junction. It loops sense → decide → act about twenty times a second until it reaches the goal, recording each turn. Then it radios the finished route to a second robot that runs it perfectly. The students wrote all of the code; the maze is a classroom-sized version of how warehouse robots and robot vacuums navigate.

04 How it works — the loop

Sense

Read distance to the left, right and front walls in millimetres from the time-of-flight sensors.

Classify

Turn raw mm into simple facts: is the left open? the right? is the front clear? (open = far enough away).

Decide

Apply the priority chain LEFT → FORWARD → RIGHT → BACK — take the first option that's open.

Act

Turn, drive one cell, record the move, and repeat — until the goal, then broadcast the whole route.

05 Why it always solves it

The left-hand rule

If you walk through a maze always keeping your left hand touching the wall, you are guaranteed to trace every corridor and reach the exit. Our priority order LEFT → FORWARD → RIGHT → BACK encodes exactly that.

The guarantee

For a "perfect" maze (no closed loops), the walls form one connected surface — so a wall-follower visits every cell and must reach the goal. We also cap the number of moves as a safety net.

Route notation broadcast over radio: L = left, R = right, F = forward, B = back. e.g. L F F R L.

07 Glossary

micro:bit V2
A small classroom computer with a processor, LED screen and built-in radio. It runs our code.
CutebotPro
The two-wheeled robot car the micro:bit plugs into and drives.
Time-of-Flight (ToF)
A sensor that measures distance by timing a laser pulse's round-trip — gives millimetres.
PWM
Pulse-width modulation — how we set each motor's speed precisely.
Junction
A point where the robot must choose a direction (a turn or a crossroads).
Perfect maze
A maze with exactly one path between any two points — no loops.
Left-hand rule
Always keep one hand on the wall; guaranteed to reach the exit.
Flood-fill
A smarter future algorithm that finds the shortest path, not just any path.

08 Likely questions & answers

How does it find its way with no map?
It keeps its "left hand" on the wall. Following that one rule traces every corridor of the maze and is mathematically guaranteed to reach the exit — no map, no GPS, no memory required.
What exactly did the students build?
All of the software: reading and interpreting the sensors, the decision logic that picks each turn, the motor control that drives and turns accurately, and the radio system that hands a solved route to a second robot. The hardware is standard kit; the cleverness is the code.
Does it always solve the maze?
Yes. The left-hand rule is provably guaranteed on a "perfect" maze (one with no closed loops). As an extra safety net, the code caps the number of moves so a robot can never run forever.
How much does it cost?
It uses a standard micro:bit V2 and a CutebotPro — roughly £70–90 per robot — which is reused across classes year after year. No specialist or expensive hardware.
Why are there two robots?
Exploring is slow and uncertain, but a solved maze is just a list of turns. One robot explores and records the route; it radios that route to a second robot, which replays the known-good path at full speed. Explore once, run forever.
What did the students learn?
Coding and logical thinking, debugging real hardware (noisy sensors, drifting motors — not just code on a screen), teamwork by splitting one system into parts, and perseverance through many failed runs before it worked reliably.
How does this connect to the curriculum?
It's hands-on computational thinking: decomposing a big problem, solving each part, and testing. It links computing, maths (distances, thresholds, geometry) and physics (sensors, motion) to a motivating real-world outcome.
What's next for the project?
A flood-fill algorithm for shortest-path solving, larger and harder mazes, timed speed-runs of a known route, and a swarm of robots that share maps over radio to solve a maze together.
What happens if a sensor misreads?
We stop the robot at each junction so the lasers settle before reading, which removes most noise. The thresholds also have margin, and the move-cap stops any runaway behaviour.
Could it get lost or stuck in a loop?
Not with the left-hand rule on a perfect maze — it's guaranteed to progress. If we add loops to mazes in future, we'll switch to flood-fill, which tracks where it has been.
