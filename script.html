What to say, slide by slide

Aim for ~50 seconds a slide. These are prompts, not a word-for-word read — say it naturally. Julian G leads; everyone narrates a piece of the live demo. Pause and breathe on the demo.

01Cover — Maze RunnerJulian G

Good afternoon. We're Abhi, Julian, Julian and Sam — and this is Maze Runner, a robot we built that solves a maze it has never seen before. In the next twenty minutes we'll show you how it works — and then it'll solve a real maze, live.

02The teamEveryone
Abhi: I worked on the decision logic — the brain that picks each turn.
Julian C: I handled the sensors and tuning.
Julian G: I built the run loop and the radio.
Sam: and I did the testing, the motors, and the demo.
03Why it mattersJulian G

Why does this matter? A robot that finds its way somewhere new, with no map, is the same idea behind warehouse robots, Mars rovers, and the vacuum at home. We built a small, honest version — and we'll prove it works in front of you.

04The missionJulian G

Here's the challenge. The robot starts completely blind — no map, no GPS. All it gets is three numbers: how far the wall is to its left, right and front. From those alone it has to decide every turn until it finds the goal.

05The hardwareSam

It's built from standard kit. A micro:bit — a little classroom computer — sits on a two-wheeled robot car. Three laser sensors measure the walls, and the built-in radio lets it share the answer. Nothing exotic — the cleverness is all in the code.

06The cast (architecture)Julian C

We split the software into four jobs: eyes that read the sensors, a brain that decides, legs that drive, and a voice that broadcasts the route. A small main program conducts the four of them.

07How it seesJulian C

Each sensor fires a laser and times the bounce — and that becomes a distance in millimetres. A wall reads small, about a hundred; an open corridor reads big, over three hundred. So "open" or "wall" is just a number compared to a threshold.

08The heartbeat (loop)Abhi

The whole robot is one loop, running about twenty times a second: sense the walls, classify them, decide a turn, act. Sense, decide, act — over and over, until it's solved.

09The decision, in codeAbhi

Here's the actual decision. If the corridor ahead is clear, it just cruises. At a junction it stops and asks: is this the goal? If not, it picks a direction — by priority.

10The strategy — left-hand ruleJulian G

And the priority is the magic. It's the left-hand rule: always keep your left hand on the wall. Left first, then forward, then right, and only turn back at a dead end. Follow that and you are mathematically guaranteed to reach the exit of any maze.

11★ LIVE DEMOAll four
Julian G: Let's see it. This is our real maze — the same one on screen and on the floor.
Abhi: Watch the panel — it senses the three walls…
Julian C: …runs down its priority list and rejects the closed paths…
Sam: …and drives. The pink line is its trail — and there's the goal.

CUE: press A on the robot to run the real maze · Random Maze on screen shows it works on any layout · press B to stop.

12The dials (tuning)Julian C

Real sensors are noisy and motors drift, so we calibrated these numbers by hand — the distance that counts as "open", the trim that keeps it driving straight. Small tweaks, big difference.

13The thought processAbhi

A few design choices worth a mention: we stop to decide so the lasers give clean readings; we trim one motor to cancel drift; and we record every move, so a second robot can replay it.

14Explore once, run foreverSam

That's the clever part — explore once, run forever. The first robot solves it slowly and records the turns, then radios them to a second robot, which runs the route at full speed without thinking.

15Problems we had to beatAbhi

It wasn't smooth at first. The sensors read rubbish while moving, so we stop at each junction. The motors drifted, so we trimmed them. And a naïve rule can loop forever — the left-hand rule fixed that for good.

16What it achievesJulian G

The result: it solves the maze a hundred percent of the time, making about twenty decisions a second, then hands the route to a second robot. Explore once, replay forever.

17Where this idea goesSam

And it's not just a toy. The same loop runs warehouse robots, search-and-rescue robots in places with no map, and the robot vacuum on your floor.

18Skills we builtSam

Building it, we learned to code and think logically, to debug real hardware — not just code on a screen — to work as a team, and to keep going through a lot of failed runs.

19Good for every studentJulian C

For school, it turns abstract lessons into something you can watch move — it links maths, computing and physics — and it runs on affordable kit any class can reuse.

20Where we take it nextJulian G

Next, we want it to find the shortest path, take on bigger mazes, do timed speed-runs, and eventually a whole swarm of robots sharing maps over radio.

21Thank youJulian G

That's Maze Runner — three sensors, one rule, a solved maze. Thank you for watching. We'd love to take your questions.

22Q&AEveryone

We're happy to answer anything — how it works, what we learned, or where it goes next.
