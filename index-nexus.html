the mission

Solve a maze it has
never seen before

The robot starts blind. There's no map, no GPS, no memory of the maze. All it has is three numbers — how far the wall is to its left, right, and front, measured in millimetres.

From those three numbers alone it must decide, junction by junction, which way to turn — and keep going until it spots the goal. Then it replays the whole route over radio so a second robot can run it perfectly.

how it sees

From laser pulse
to a decision

Each sensor fires an invisible laser pulse and times how long it takes to bounce back. That time becomes a distance in millimetres.

A nearby wall reads small — around 110 mm. An open corridor reads large — 330 mm or more. One number per direction is all the brain needs.

left  330 mm front 110 mm right 330 mm
the rule of thumb

open vs wall

> 300 mm on a side → an opening you can turn into.

> 170 mm ahead → clear to drive forward.

Anything less → a wall.

a corridor cell ≈ 220 mm wide
the heartbeat

A basic.pause(50) at the bottom paces the loop so sensors settle and motors respond — the robot "thinks" in calm, deliberate ticks rather than a frantic blur.

the strategy

The left-hand rule

"Always keep your left hand on the wall." Follow that one rule and you will trace every corridor of a simply-connected maze — and you are guaranteed to find the exit. Our priority order encodes exactly that bias.

Each junction is resolved by the first option that's actually open, top to bottom:

the dials

Tuned by hand, in millimetres

Real sensors are noisy and motors drift. These constants are the calibration that turns physics into reliable behaviour.

FRONT_CLEAR_MM170How much space ahead counts as "keep driving."
SIDE_OPEN_MM300A side reading beyond this means an opening — a turn is possible.
GOAL_SIDE_MM400Wide-open sides: a tell-tale sign of the goal chamber.
DRIVE_SPEED25Base PWM cruise speed for both motors.
MOTOR_BIAS+6Right motor trim — cancels the robot's natural drift so it tracks straight.
BLIND_DRIVE_CM15Distance to nudge forward into a junction before re-reading.
ORIENT_BAIL_MM65If a wall is this close while aligning, abort the orient to avoid a crash.
MAX_MOVES5Safety cap — stop after N decisions so a lost robot never runs forever.
the thought process

Why it's built this way

?

Why stop to decide?

Time-of-flight sensors read garbage while the robot is mid-turn or accelerating. Halting at each junction gives clean, settled numbers — accuracy beats raw speed when one bad reading sends you down the wrong corridor.

?

Why a motor bias instead of perfect motors?

No two motors are identical. Rather than fight it, we measured the drift once and bake in +6 on the right motor — cheap, robust, and it makes "drive straight" actually go straight.

?

Why log every move into rawRoute?

Exploring is slow and uncertain. But once solved, the path is just a list of turns. We record the route as we go, then a second robot replays it flawlessly at full speed — explore once, exploit forever.

?

Why orient & centre flags?

DO_ORIENT squares the robot to the walls; DO_CENTRE keeps it mid-corridor. They're toggleable so we can isolate and debug one behaviour at a time on real hardware.

the hard parts

Problems we had to beat

problem 01

Noisy lasers

Sensors read garbage while moving. Fix: stop dead at every junction so the readings settle before we trust them — accuracy over speed.

problem 02

Motors drift

No two motors are identical, so "straight" curves. Fix: we measured the drift once and trim the right motor by +6 forever.

problem 03

Getting stuck

A naïve turn order can loop forever. Fix: the true left-hand rule is mathematically guaranteed to find the exit of any maze.

Each fix came from watching the real robot fail — then reasoning about why.

questions

Ask us anything

Q.How does it navigate with no map?
It keeps its "left hand" on the wall — a rule proven to reach the exit of any solid maze. No map needed.
Q.What did the students build?
All of the code: reading the sensors, the decision logic, the motor control, and the radio hand-off between robots.
Q.Does it always solve it?
Yes — the left-hand rule is mathematically guaranteed to reach the goal of a perfect maze. We also cap moves as a safety net.
Q.Is the kit expensive?
No — a standard micro:bit V2 and a CutebotPro (~£70–90 per robot), reused across classes year after year.
Q.Why two robots?
One explores and records the route; it radios that route to a second robot which replays it at full speed — explore once, run forever.
Q.What's next?
Shortest-path (flood-fill) solving, bigger mazes, timed speed-runs, and a swarm sharing maps over radio.
